TOV equations

The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are the relativistic equations for a static, spherically symmetric star (fluid ball) in hydrostatic equilibrium. The Einstein equations and hydrodynamic equations are simultaneously solved to find the solutions. The derivations of the equations often starts from an exponential form of the metric and makes a Schwarzschild like mass substitution near the end. It is as illustrative to start directly from the mass form of the metric g_{\mu\nu} = -e^{2\Phi(r)}dt^2 + \frac{dr^2}{1-\frac{2m(r)}{r}} + r^2\left(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta d\phi^2\right)

The Einstein equations

The stress energy tensor is derived from the above metric and the normal stress energy tensor for a perfect fluid, given in terms of the total energy density of the fluid \rho, the pressure p and the four velocity of the fluid element u^a: T_{ab} = \rho u_au_b + p\left(u_au_b + g_{ab} \right) In a static spacetime with the metric above, the four velocity of the fluid follows the integral curves of the time coordinate u^\mu = e^{-\Phi(r)}\partial_t \equiv \left(e^{-\Phi(r)},0,0,0 \right) or as a 1-form u_\mu = -e^{\Phi(r)}dt \equiv \left(-e^{\Phi(r)},0,0,0 \right) Therefore, the stress energy tensor is given in terms of the variables as T_{\mu\nu} = \rho e^{2\Phi} dt^2 + \frac{Pdr^2}{1-\frac{2m}{r}} +Pr^2\left(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta d\phi^2 \right).

Using the metric and stress energy tensor, the Einstein equations G_{ab} = 8\pi T_{ab} can be computed. This yields two equations \frac{dm}{dr}=4\pi r^2\rho and \frac{d\Phi}{dr}=\frac{m + 4 \pi r^3 p}{r^2 - 2 r m}

Conservation of stress energy

The divergence of the stress energy can be readily computed from the mixed form of the stress energy tensor \nabla_b T_a{}^b = \nabla_b \left(\rho u_a u^b + p(u_a u^b + \delta_a{}^b)\right) This equation can be expanded and the covariant derivatives of scalar fields replaced with partial derivatives to yield \nabla_b T_a{}^b = u_a u^b \partial_b (\rho + p) + (\rho + p)\nabla_b u_a u^b + \partial_a p Next the covariant derivatives can be expanded in place of their definitions in terms of the Christoffel symbols \nabla_b T_a{}^b = u_a u^b \partial_b (\rho + p) + (\rho + p) \left(\partial_b u_a u^b - \Gamma^c{}_{ab} u_c u^b + \Gamma^b{}_{bc} u_a u^c\right) + \partial_a p For the TOV equations, the only non-vanishing part of the four-velocity is the t component, while the only variable that the fields depend upon is r. This leaves the following equation \partial_r p - (\rho + p) \Gamma^t{}_{rt} u_t u^t + (\rho + p) \Gamma^\mu{}_{\mu t} u_t u^t = 0 Here the multiplication of the covariant and contravariant component of the four velocity yield -1 simplifying the expression to \partial_r p + (\rho + p) \Gamma^t{}_{rt} - (\rho + p) \Gamma^b{}_{bt} = 0 The only non-vanishing Christoffel symbol is the {t,r,t} component (equal to the radial derivative of the lapse field phi), so the equation for hydrostatic equilibrium is \frac{dP}{dr} = -(\rho + p) \frac{d\Phi}{dr} = -(\rho + p) \frac{m + 4 \pi r^3 p}{r^2 - 2 r m}.

Boundary conditions

The problem of solving the forgoing equations for a spherical star defines a free boundary problem, where the radius of the star is unknown. The inner boundary conditions for m and p are straightforward for an initial value problem, while the boundary condition for \Phi arises by requiring that at the surface of the star r_s the metric matches the Schwarzschild metric: m(0) = 0, p(0) = p_c, p(r_s) = 0, \Phi(r_s) = \frac{1}{2} \ln \left( 1 - \frac{ 2 m(r_s) }{ r_s } \right). The central pressure p_c is a free parameter of the problem. If solving the equations using integration from r = 0 \rightarrow r_s it is easier to solve with \Phi(0) = 0 and integrate the entire solution and then find a constant value which can be added to \Phi to satisfy the surface boundary condition.

Equations of state

Realistic equations of state are research problems in and of themself and generally result in tables (often requiring ad hoc "smoothing" treatments). There are however two common closed form equations of state used in structural studies.

The first closed form equation of state is the simplest, the constant density equation of state. For this equation of state, the total energy density is constant and not a function of pressure or radius \rho(p) = \rho_c.

The second closed form equation of state is known as the polytropic equation of state. It is usually given in two parts, first the adiabatic pressure term p = K (m_b n)^\gamma = K \rho_0{}^\gamma with m_b the average rest mass per particle (baryon), n the number density of particles, \rho_0 the rest mass density, and K and \gamma are constants. The second term is the ideal gas relation for the specific internal energy, \epsilon = \frac{p}{\rho_0(1 - \gamma)} which gives the relation for the total energy density \rho = \rho_0(1 + \epsilon) = \rho_0 + \frac{K \rho_0{}^\gamma}{\gamma - 1}. This latter relation can also be derived from the first law of thermodynamics for an adiabatic fluid d\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_0}\right) = -Pd\left(\frac{1}{\rho_0}\right) by choosing the constant of integration properly (by requiring \lim_{\rho_0 \rightarrow 0} \rho / \rho_0 = 1).
